Guided Visualization Script Instructions
Creative Goal

Write stories that feel alive, cinematic, weird and strange in the best way.
Every story should grab attention immediately, move fast, and end with a surprising but satisfying emotional truth.

The stories should have a lot of scene and object shifts, like the old spice ad "I'm on a horse".

The listener should feel both grounded in reality and transported into a surreal dream.

Tone: playful, vivid, confident, emotionally real.

Length: 600–700 words.

1. Opening Hook

Start with a line that surprises.
Something unexpected, thought-provoking, or slightly absurd — yet emotionally tied to the story’s theme.
It should instantly trigger curiosity.

After the first 1–2 sentences, say:
“If you haven’t already, it’s time to close your eyes.”
"It's time to close your eyes"
or similar.

Then, begin to move the listener through action and sensation. The world should feel familiar but twisted.

2. The Curiosity Loop

The hook must tie to the core theme — courage, calm, change, freedom, forgiveness, joy, etc.
Something strange happens early, and by the end, it’s revealed to mean something simple and true.

Example:

Hook: “You wake up to find your name written in the sky.”
Resolution: “You realize you were always meant to take up space.”

This loop creates an emotional throughline and makes the absurd meaningful.

3. Language

Always write in second person (“you”).

Use short, confident sentences.

Keep verbs strong and clear — walk, lift, breathe, turn, open, run, float, laugh.

Cut filler words: no almost, kind of, seems, slightly, just enough.

Describe what is happening, not what might be.

❌ “It feels like the sky might be shifting.”
✅ “The sky shifts.”

Keep tone cool, cinematic, and direct.

4. Scene Transitions (Rapid Cuts)

Change scenes or objects every 2–4 sentences.
Transitions should be fast, smooth, and surprising — like dream logic or a music video.

Example chain:

“You step into the elevator. The doors close.
When they open, you’re underwater.
You swim upward. The surface breaks into sunlight.
You look down, you’re standing in a field of golden sand.”

Each transition should feel natural in rhythm but visually bold.
Every change moves the listener closer to an emotional realization.

5. The Symbol

Each story has one recurring symbol — an object, sound, or phrase that follows the listener through transformations.

The symbol evolves clearly:

Appears in a surprising way.

Changes form or use mid-story.

Becomes the story’s truth at the end.

Examples:

A ringing phone → a heartbeat → a pulse of courage.

A feather → a compass → a truth about direction.

A door → a horizon → a choice.

By the final paragraph, the meaning of the symbol should land emotionally and complete the story’s opening hook.

6. Sensory Immersion

Keep sensations concrete and active: sight, sound, touch, smell, temperature.
Each short scene uses a new sense or combination.

Examples:

“The air smells like oranges.”

“You feel sand sliding between your fingers.”

“You smell rain before it falls.”

“Your shoes squeak on polished marble.”

Every line should let the listener feel inside the world.

7. Emotional Arc

Follow a fast, clear arc — like a 5-minute short film:

Tension / Curiosity: The strange beginning.

Transformation: The world keeps shifting — each new scene reveals a bit more truth.

Realization: A sharp, emotional “click.” The absurd becomes meaningful.

Release: Calm, clarity, empowerment.

End with resolution of the opening hook.
Bring the listener full circle — same theme, new understanding.

8. Pacing

Think in beats — every few sentences, a cut or revelation.
Use rhythm to control emotion:

Fast for surprise or action.

Slow for awe or calm.

Stop for emotional clarity.

“You stop. Everything holds still.
The world waits for your next breath.”

Avoid soft pacing; keep the story moving forward.

9. Hypnotic Suggestions

Blend them naturally into action, without breaking tone.

Examples:

“Each breath steadies you.”

“You walk faster, feeling strength rise inside you.”

“You laugh, and the sound unlocks something bright in your chest.”

Keep phrasing direct and embodied, not abstract.

10. Grounding and Return

End decisively. Bring the listener back with sensory action.

“You breathe in. Feel your hands, your feet, your heartbeat.
The world is still here — brighter now.
When you’re ready, open your eyes.”

Always close with a clear, uplifting image that ties back to the hook.

“And somewhere, the phone rings again — but this time, you already know what it’s saying.”

✅ Final Creative Checklist (Pro Mode v5)

 Story opens with an unexpected, curiosity-grabbing hook.

 “If you haven’t already, it’s time to close your eyes” follows early.

 Every sentence uses clear, active verbs.

 Scene and object changes happen rapidly (every 2–4 sentences).

 One symbol evolves through three stages and ties to the theme.

 Each transition feels surprising but seamless.

 Emotional arc is tight (Curiosity → Transformation → Realization → Release).

 Tone mixes relatable and absurd — playful but sincere.

 Ending resolves the hook and grounds the listener in the body.

Use simple easy to understand language.

Give the story a title.
